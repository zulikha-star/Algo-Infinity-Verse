Understanding the Tortoise and Hare

The Fast and Slow pointer technique, also known as Floyd's Cycle-Finding Algorithm, uses two pointers moving at different speeds to detect cycles or find middle elements in a linked list or sequence.

Why does it work for Cycle Detection?

Imagine two runners on a track. If the track is a straight line, the faster runner will reach the end. But if the track has a loop, the faster runner will eventually lap the slower runner. Mathematically, the relative speed between the fast pointer (2 steps) and slow pointer (1 step) is 1 step per iteration. Therefore, the distance between them decreases by 1 each step, guaranteeing they will eventually meet if a cycle exists.

Finding the Cycle Entry Point

Once the pointers meet, if you move one pointer back to the head of the list and keep both moving at 1 step per iteration, they will meet exactly at the cycle's entry point.
